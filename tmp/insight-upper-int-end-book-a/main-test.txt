A floating school
By founder, Anna Domingo
I coordinate a floating school which I established several years ago. It is a large motor boat that
can carry a classful of students, and my aim was to transport educational materials, such as books
and tablets, to young people who live on a remote island without school buildings or resident
teachers. Life here can feel isolated, partly due to the lack of internet. While some young people
travel to the mainland for continuing education, this can be inconvenient to their lifestyles,
especially for those of primary-school age.
We take education to students and we have developed a curriculum of subjects which suits life on
the island, that varies significantly from the national one used on the mainland. Our programme is
more informal and focused around what is relevant to our students’ lives. For example, many
young people are involved in fishing which is necessary to help support their families. Typical
education follows a national curriculum which doesn’t cover the maritime or fishing skills required
by such students. We try to bridge that gap by teaching relevant skills.
So, how does it work? Those in the fishing industry, around isolated islands or otherwise, are well
aware of the need to fish in a sustainable manner and avoid destructive practices which might
damage marine life. It isn’t that there’s a lack of understanding about these matters, but we can
fill in knowledge gaps and provide relevant up-to-date information on the protection of
ecosystems, and skills and techniques to support this. It isn’t up to us to tell people how to run
their industry, but to facilitate practical learning about what matters for the future of our oceans.
Our teaching involves people we call ‘field experts’. These are volunteers who spend some of
their free time travelling to the island to talk about their professions. We have a good database
now, including doctors, lawyers, engineers, journalists, musicians … you name it! There’s usually a
relatively brief presentation followed by a question-and-answer session about their roles. Those
students who do go on to further education often take the route of one of the professionals
they’ve talked to, and many adapt their university courses to maintain a connection with the
island. For example, a medical worker might focus on traditional medicine.
Like anything, it takes time for new ideas to take hold, and we’ve gradually grown to around 200
students. It’s hugely satisfying to have seen students all the way through to university and hear
them say how keen they are to remain in their island community to give something back through
their own learning with us. Sometimes people ask me how we’ll continue to develop. To be
honest, I think we’ve got a winning format, so for the time being, we’ll continue to deliver our
programmes just as they are, responding to change if necessary.
Of course, If I’d known what I know now at the start, I’d have done some things differently but on
the whole, I’m thrilled to hear that others in a similar situation are looking at what we’ve done
and using our model as a starting point for their work. That’s a massive vote of confidence in our
work from those working in challenging circumstances who might never have thought it possible.
